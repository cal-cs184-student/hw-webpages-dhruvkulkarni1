Overview

In this homework we built a cloth simulator from the ground up. We started by representing the cloth as a grid of point masses connected by springs of three kinds: structural (resist stretching), shearing (resist in-plane distortion), and bending (resist folding). Then we ran Verlet integration on the masses with gravity and Hooke's law spring forces, plus a Provot length constraint to keep the cloth from stretching too much in one step. After that, we added collisions with spheres and planes, and self-collisions using a spatial hash. Finally we wrote the shaders: diffuse, Blinn-Phong, texture mapping, bump mapping, displacement mapping, and an environment-mapped mirror.

The coolest part for us was realizing how many moving pieces have to line up before the cloth looks right. The springs give it shape, Verlet moves it over time, Provot keeps it from blowing up, and collisions stop it from passing through things. None of those parts are very fancy on their own, but stacking them on top of each other is what makes it work. We also noticed that a lot of what makes the final images look realistic actually comes from the shading, not the physics. A flat shaded cloth without shadows or highlights feels way less convincing than the same cloth with Blinn-Phong applied.

Part 1: Masses and springs

We implemented Cloth::buildGrid in two passes. First we placed all point masses into a row-major grid, with the index for a mass at column i and row j being j * num_width_points + i. For horizontal cloths, we put each mass at (i*dx, 1, j*dy). For vertical cloths, we put them at (i*dx, j*dy, rand) where rand is a small random number between -1/1000 and 1/1000 to seed a bit of asymmetry. Each mass gets checked against the pinned list and marked if it's in there.

We create the springs in a second pass, after every mass has been pushed into the vector. This matters because springs store raw pointers into point_masses, and if the vector grew while we were making springs, those pointers could become invalid. Each mass connects to up to six neighbors: two structural (left and up), two shearing (the two upper diagonals), and two bending (two cells left and two cells up). Edge bounds are checked so masses on the boundary don't try to connect to non-existent neighbors.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

Each step, we first reset every mass's force to gravity times its mass. Then we loop over the enabled springs and apply Hooke's law: the force magnitude is ks * (current_length - rest_length), in the direction of the spring. We push +F on one endpoint and -F on the other. Bending springs use 0.2 * ks instead of the full ks, since they're meant to resist folding more gently than structural springs resist stretching.

Next, we run Verlet on every non-pinned mass. The update is \( x_{t+\Delta t} = x_t + (1 - d)(x_t - x_{t-\Delta t}) + a_t \Delta t^2 \), where d is the damping percent divided by 100. The previous position acts like a stored velocity, so we don't need to keep a velocity vector separately. After integration, we run the Provot constraint: for every spring, if its length is more than 1.1 times the rest length, we pull the endpoints together until it's exactly 1.1 times. If both endpoints are unpinned, each one moves by half the correction. If one is pinned, the other one takes the full correction.

Varying the spring constant ks

With ks = 100, the cloth is very stretchy and sags way below the pinned corners. The Provot clamp is basically what keeps it stable, since the springs themselves are too weak to hold the cloth up. With ks = 5000 (the default) it behaves like regular fabric. At ks = 50000, the cloth becomes stiff. It barely sags at all and looks more like a rigid board than a piece of fabric.

Varying density

Density is the mass per unit area. At density = 1 the cloth is very light, so gravity has a harder time pulling it down against the springs. At the default density = 15 it drapes normally. At density = 100 every mass is way heavier, so the cloth stretches a lot more and the Provot clamp kicks in for more springs.

Varying damping

Damping scales the implicit velocity term in Verlet, so it controls how much energy the cloth loses each step. At damping = 0 the cloth is basically a perfectly elastic system and oscillates forever. At the default damping = 0.2 it settles within a few seconds. At damping = 1.0 it's close to over-damped and moves slowly toward its rest shape without bouncing around.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

For spheres, we first check if the mass is inside the sphere by comparing |pos - origin| to the radius. If it is, we find the tangent point on the surface by taking the origin plus the radius along the unit vector from origin to mass. Then we move the mass from its last_position toward that tangent, scaled by (1 - friction). We start the correction from last_position instead of position because we assume the mass was outside the sphere last step, so starting from there makes sure we end up outside too.

For planes, we compute the signed distance of both last_position and position using dot(p - point, normal). If those two have different signs, the mass crossed the plane. We find the intersection point along that line segment, then offset it by SURFACE_OFFSET along the same side as last_position so the corrected position sits just above the plane. Same (1 - friction) scaling as the sphere.

Effect of ks on sphere drape

At ks = 500 the cloth is very stretchy, so it hangs way past the sphere and gathers a lot at the bottom. At ks = 5000 it drapes naturally over the top and hangs off with a few folds. At ks = 50000 the cloth is so stiff that it barely conforms to the sphere. It sits more like a rigid cap with the edges jutting out.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

To avoid checking every pair of masses each step, we used spatial hashing. Our hash_position function divides space into boxes of size w x h x t, where w = 3 * width / num_width_points, h = 3 * height / num_height_points, and t = max(w, h). For a given position, we floor each coordinate divided by its box size to get cell indices, and combine them into a single float using (cx * 31 + cy) * 31 + cz. The 31s are just prime multipliers so different cells don't collide on the hash too often.

build_spatial_map runs once per timestep. It walks through point_masses and pushes each one into the bucket for its hash. self_collide looks up the bucket for a given mass and checks every other mass in it. If the distance between them is less than 2 * thickness, we compute a correction that pushes them apart. We sum all these corrections, divide by the count (so one mass doesn't get pushed too far), then divide by simulation_steps so the correction is gentle across substeps. Using cell sizes that are 3x the mass spacing gives us enough slack that most candidate colliders end up in the same bucket, which is why we don't need to check the 26 neighboring cells.

Falling progression

Effect of density on folding

Low density makes the cloth light, so it doesn't have enough energy to push itself into tight folds. It ends up looking looser and fluffier. Higher density drives it down harder, so the final shape is flatter and the fall produces more aggressive folding along the way.

Effect of ks on folding

With a low ks, the cloth is floppy and folds into a bunch of small creases. The springs barely resist anything. With a very high ks, the cloth acts more like a rigid sheet, so instead of crumpling it tips over and stacks in a few big angular folds.

Part 5: Shaders

A shader program runs on the GPU and is made up of at least two stages. The vertex shader runs once per vertex and outputs its screen-space position plus any per-vertex attributes we want to pass along. Those outputs get interpolated across the triangle and handed to the fragment shader, which runs once per pixel and computes the final color. For this homework, we mostly wrote fragment shaders that implement lighting models, plus one vertex shader (displacement) that moves the geometry itself.

Blinn-Phong

The Blinn-Phong model has three parts: \( L = k_a I_a + k_d (I/r^2) \max(0, n\cdot\ell) + k_s (I/r^2) \max(0, n\cdot h)^p \), where h = normalize(l + v) is the half-vector between the light and view directions. The ambient term makes sure shadowed parts aren't pitch black, diffuse gives the matte response that depends on the angle to the light, and specular produces the sharp highlight where the surface is pointed toward the viewer relative to the light.

Texture mapping

Texture mapping reads a color from a 2D image at the UV coordinate of each fragment. Ours is basically one line: out_color = texture(u_texture_1, v_uv). For the custom texture, we used a desert tree photo that gets wrapped across the cloth.

Bump vs. displacement mapping

Bump mapping fakes surface detail by perturbing the normal used for lighting. We take finite differences of the height map to get du and dv, build a tangent-space normal as (-du, -dv, 1), transform it into world space using the TBN matrix, and feed that into Blinn-Phong. The geometry doesn't actually change at all, we just lie about the normal.

Displacement mapping actually moves the vertex outward along its normal in the vertex shader, by h(uv) * height_scaling. So the geometry itself gets bumpy. The easy way to tell the two apart is to look at the silhouette from a side angle. Bump maps keep a smooth outline since the geometry is flat, while displacement maps produce an actually rough silhouette.

Mirror (environment-mapped reflections)

For the mirror shader, we take the view direction from the camera to the surface, reflect it across the surface normal, and sample the scene cubemap in that reflected direction. The result is a chrome look that picks up whatever surrounds the object in the cubemap.
